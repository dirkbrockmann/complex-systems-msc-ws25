The Kuramoto model of coupled oscillators

In this script we discuss the Kuramoto Model. It is probably the most famous model for the emergence of synchronization in phase coupled oscillators. It was introduced in 1975 by Yoshiki Kuramoto.

Although it is conceptually extremely simple, in captures the basic mechanism of spontaneous synchronization of interacting oscillators, it explains why synchronization sometimes occurs and sometimes doesn’t. Despite it’s formal simplicity it hold a number of unexpected surprises when many oscillators are involved. One such surprise is the existence of so called chimera states in which in a large set of oscillators a subset synchronizes perfectly while others fail to do so.

When the Kuramoto model is discussed in the literature, one often begins with the equations, followed by an intuitive and plausible interpretation which is perfectly fine. Yet, when that is done I feel it masks how the equations can be derived from scratch, following the guidelines of simplicity and plausibility. So, in the discussion below we will try to start from scratch and pay a bit more attention to the construction of the model. So, bear with me.

Introduction

First, let’s think about oscillators. The defining feature of an oscillator is the repetition of behavior. So we have a dynamic state of the system that repeats and repeats and repeats. Nature abounds in processes that are oscillatory, rhythmic or repetitive.

If we think of systems that change their state continuously in time and that the state of the system at any time ttt can be described by an NNN-dimensional state vector X(t)=(X1(t),...,XN(t))\mathbf X(t)=(X_1(t),...,X_N(t))X(t)=(X1​(t),...,XN​(t)) rhythmic / oscillatory / periodic behavior means that

X(t)=X(t+T).\mathbf X(t) = \mathbf X(t+T).X(t)=X(t+T).

So after a period TTT the state is where it started off and begins the journey along the periodic trajectory again. Geometrically you can think of the periodic motion as curve in the NNN-dimensional state space. This geometric idea is very helpful. Because it means that we can geometrically map the periodic trajectory, even if it looks complicated and potentially with a weird shape in state space onto a nicely shape circle, with every point on the periodic trajectory corresponding to a point on the circle. Just like we can have a rubberband shaped in weird ways to be reshaped into a nice circular shape. No matter what weird shape the rubber band is in, it remains a circular object.

So in a sense, the geometry of the problem isn’t important, rather the fraction of the period TTT that has passed. And that fraction of the period we are going to call the phase θ(t)\theta(t)θ(t) of an oscillator.

Thinking again of the periodic behavior in the simplest way possible, that of a point moving around a circle (the radius of which doesn’t matter), the phase is just the angle, say with the x-axis. Where we place θ=0\theta=0θ=0 doesn’t matter, because frankly, a circle doesn’t have a beginning or an end.

The Phase

So, bottomline: An oscillator can be described, abstractly, as an angle variable θ(t)\theta(t)θ(t) that moves around a circle. It doesn’t have to do it at a constant speed. In some circle regions it could move faster than in others. It could even come to a stop (stretching the idea of an oscillator a bit).

Because we have this motion on a circle, it also means that the motion has no beginning and no end. If we want to measure the state, and attach a number to it, we need to set a point on the circle that corresponds to θ=0\theta=0θ=0, as mentioned earlier. If we embed the circle in the plane with our conventional placement of xxx- and yyy-axis, the beginning is usually placed at the cartesian point (1,0)(1,0)(1,0) and increasing θ\thetaθ is happening in the counterclockwise way 1 .

1It is a convention to define the angle with respect to the x-axis and increasing the angle means moving the state in a counterclockwise fashion. Pure convention. You could start anywhere and move in any direction.

This is all very straightforward. An oscillator performs dynamics on a ring. Yet there are a few traps connected to the fact that a ring has no beginning and no end. Formally, we let

θ∈[0,2π)\theta\in [0,2\pi)θ∈[0,2π)

So, when we do algebra with angles like the state θ\thetaθ we need to be careful. So for instance if by an algebraic operation we jump over the boundary at 000. If, e.g., we say θ=359°\theta=359\degreeθ=359° and define an increment δθ=5°\delta\theta=5\degreeδθ=5° the sum

θ+δθ=4°\theta+\delta\theta = 4\degreeθ+δθ=4°

which would suggest that δθ=−355°\delta\theta=-355\degreeδθ=−355°. But of course on a circle −355°=5°-355\degree=5\degree−355°=5°. We just have to keep that in mind. Seems trivial, but could lead to mistakes if one doesn’t keep it in mind.

Here’s an example that illustrates it. Let’s say we have two angles θ1=50°\theta_1=50\degreeθ1​=50° and θ2=80°\theta_2=80\degreeθ2​=80° and we would like to compute the average of both angles:

<θ>=12(θ1+θ2)\left< \theta\right>=\frac{1}{2}(\theta_1+\theta_2)⟨θ⟩=21​(θ1​+θ2​)

As expected we get <θ>=65°\left< \theta\right>=65\degree⟨θ⟩=65° which, on the circle this is halfway between both angles. The difference between the angles in this case

θ2−θ1=30°\theta_2-\theta_1=30\degreeθ2​−θ1​=30°

Let’s compute the average of two angles θ1=350°\theta_1=350\degreeθ1​=350° and θ2=20°\theta_2=20\degreeθ2​=20°. This amounts to <θ>=185°\left< \theta\right>=185\degree⟨θ⟩=185°. This is a little odd because, the average is no longer half way between the original angles along the short ring segment. It’s excactly opposite, in the middle of the segment that we get if we walk from θ2\theta_2θ2​ to θ1\theta_1θ1​.

Well, this might be the correct answer, but maybe not. It depends on what we mean by average angle. If we want the answer to be the angle of the line that goes through the midpoint of the arc that we get if we walk from θ1\theta_1θ1​ to θ2\theta_2θ2​, it’s not the correct answer 2 .

2One way of solving this without having to worry about the weird numerical discontinuity of the angle θ\thetaθ in computing averages is going back to Cartesian Coordinates. <θ>=tan⁡−1(<y>/<x>)\left< \theta\right>=\tan^{-1}(\left< y\right>/\left<x\right>)⟨θ⟩=tan−1(⟨y⟩/⟨x⟩)

That works.

Bottomline: Be careful with algebra on a ring.

The simplest oscillator

So the most simple oscillator is one that has a constant speed of moving around the circle:

θ˙=ω\dot{\theta} = \omega θ˙=ω

The angular ω\omegaω speed is also known as the (angular) frequency. The solution is of course

θ(t)=θ(0)+ωtmod2π.\theta(t)=\theta(0)+\omega t\quad\text{mod}\quad 2\pi.θ(t)=θ(0)+ωtmod2π.

the mod 2π2\pi2π is there to make sure that values that exceed 2π2\pi2π are mapped back in to the valid range of angles [0,2π)[0,2\pi)[0,2π).

Interacting oscillators

Now we are going to start thinking about interacting oscillators. So first let’s assume we have NNN oscillators, each with a natural frequency ωn\omega_nωn​, n=1,...,Nn=1,...,Nn=1,...,N. If they are all independent and not interacting we would have

θ˙n=ωn\dot\theta_n=\omega_nθ˙n​=ωn​

Now we introduce another type of symmetry. We assume that the interaction doesn’t care about where the oscillators are on the ring, only that relative “positions count”. So for example if I rotate all oscillators by a fixed angle, that shouldn’t matter. That implies that the interaction can only depend on phase differences:

Summary

  • if both oscillators have identical natural frequencies δω=0\delta\omega=0δω=0, they always synchronize with zero phase difference α=0\alpha=0α=0.
  • if the natural frequencies differ the oscillators synchronize only for a coupling that is larger than a critical value Kc=δωK_c=\delta\omegaKc​=δω.
  • if they synchronize they have a phase difference α=sin⁡−1(δω/K)\alpha=\sin^{-1}(\delta\omega/K)α=sin−1(δω/K).
  • in the asymptotically stable state the rate of change of both oscillators is the same and identical to the mean of their natural frequency.

Many oscillators

Naturally, the next step is considering many coupled oscillators. The generalization of the two-oscillator model comes naturally. We just assume that any pair of oscillators exert a force on each other that is identical to the two-oscillator system. So given two oscillators labeld by nnn and mmm with phase variables θn\theta_nθn​ and θm\theta_mθm​ we assume the force exerted on nnn by mmm is given by

Knm∝Knmsin⁡(θm−θn).K_{nm}\propto K_{nm}\sin(\theta_m-\theta_n).Knm​∝Knm​sin(θm​−θn​).

Now we assume that in general all oscillators have their own natural frequencies ωn\omega_nωn​ and that the forces exerted by oscillators on each other can be added. This then yields

θ˙n=ωn+1N∑m≠nKnmsin⁡(θm−θn).\dot\theta_n=\omega_n+\frac{1}{N}\sum_{m\neq n}K_{nm}\sin(\theta_m-\theta_n).θ˙n​=ωn​+N1​m=n∑​Knm​sin(θm​−θn​).

This defines the Kuramoto Model for many oscillators. The interaction matrix KnmK_{nm}Knm​ defines the interactions between oscillators.

It turns out that despite the simplicity of this model, it is quite rich in dynamical behaviors it can exhibit and it is very difficult to compute conditions for synchronization in general.
